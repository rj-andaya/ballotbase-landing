Follow the Money: The Most Honest Thing in Politics Is Who Writes the Checks

Politicians say a lot of things. Donors write checks with intent. Here's why campaign finance data is the most reliable signal in politics — and how to actually read it.

Primaries are happening across the country right now. Some wrapped up in March. Others run all the way through September. And most voters have no idea who's funding the candidates on their ballot.

That's not a knock on voters. Campaign finance data is technically public — but it's buried in government databases that are notoriously hard to navigate, updated on inconsistent schedules, and filled with raw filing data that takes real work to make readable.

This post is about why it's worth the effort. Because once you understand what the money actually tells you, it changes how you evaluate every candidate.

Every dollar over $200 is on the record

Here's something most people don't know: if a donor gives more than $200 to a federal campaign, that donation is a matter of public record. Name, employer, occupation, zip code, and amount — all of it goes into FEC filings. Most states have similar rules for state-level races.

This isn't buried in some obscure archive. It's public data, updated regularly, and it covers every serious campaign in the country.

The problem isn't availability. The problem is that the raw data is genuinely painful to work with. The FEC database alone contains hundreds of millions of records. State systems like California's Cal-Access are notorious for being difficult to navigate — the kind of thing that requires technical expertise most campaigns, journalists, and voters don't have.

So the data exists. Most people just never see it in a form that's actually useful.

The gap between what candidates say and who funds them is where the real story lives

Donor data doesn't tell you everything. But it tells you something platforms and speeches never will: where a candidate's financial relationships actually sit.

When we analyzed the 2026 California governor's race, we found two Democratic candidates running climate-forward platforms who both accepted maxed-out contributions from Chevron. Same fossil fuel company. Same $39,200 check. Two candidates publicly committed to climate action.

That's not necessarily disqualifying. Politicians build broad coalitions. Money doesn't always equal influence. But it is a real tension — and it's the kind of thing voters deserve to see before they cast a ballot.

The same pattern shows up everywhere you look. Candidates running on housing affordability with real estate developer money. Anti-corporate platforms with tech executive donors at the legal limit. Grassroots messaging backed by a single billionaire's fortune.

None of this means the candidate is lying. But it does mean the picture is more complicated than the platform says. And voters should have that picture.

The candidate's committee is just the tip of the iceberg

Here's where it gets more complicated: the money that shows up in a candidate's official fundraising totals is often not the whole story.

Independent expenditure committees — super PACs, party committees, outside groups — can spend unlimited amounts supporting or opposing candidates. They just can't coordinate directly with the campaign. These groups report their spending separately, and the total is often larger than what the candidate raised themselves.

When a candidate says "I raised $5 million," they're not counting the $8 million a super PAC spent on their behalf. When a candidate says "I have grassroots support," they might — but there's also a well-funded outside group running TV ads for them in every major market.

The full picture requires looking at both: what the candidate raised, and what was spent on their behalf. Most voters only ever see the first number.

Primaries are where money matters most

General elections get all the attention. But primaries are where donor influence is most concentrated.

Turnout in primary elections is typically a fraction of general election turnout. The electorate is smaller, more engaged, and more ideologically sorted. In that environment, money buys more — more ads, more mail, more name recognition — relative to what the campaign is competing against.

It's also the stage where candidates are most dependent on their core donor base. In a general election, a Democrat in California or a Republican in Alabama has a reliable floor of partisan votes. In a primary, they're competing for a narrower audience and more dependent on the financial infrastructure that got them on the ballot in the first place.

That dependency matters. The donors who write early checks to primary campaigns aren't just supporters — they're often the reason a candidate is viable at all.

How to actually look this stuff up

If you want to dig into a race yourself, here's where to start:

Federal races — the FEC's website (fec.gov) has filings for every federal candidate. It's functional but not easy to navigate. Search by candidate name and look at "Receipts" to see individual contributions.

California — Cal-Access (cal-access.sos.ca.gov) covers state and local races. It works, but it's not pretty.

Other states — most states have a Secretary of State or campaign finance division with a public portal. Quality varies dramatically.

The raw data will show you donor names, employers, and amounts. What takes more work is spotting the patterns — which industries show up repeatedly, how the donor profile compares to the platform, where the independent expenditure money is coming from.

The bottom line

Politicians are going to keep saying the right things. That's the job.

What changes is this: the money doesn't lie in the same way. Donors write checks because they expect something — access, alignment, a friendly regulatory environment, a candidate who shares their values. Sometimes those expectations match what the candidate is saying publicly. Sometimes they don't.

The data is there. It's public. It covers every serious candidate running for federal and state office. Most voters just never see it in a form that's useful.

That's the problem worth solving.
