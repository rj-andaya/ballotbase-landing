Nevada Campaign Finance Data Is Public. Actually Getting It Is Another Story.

Nevada's AURORA system tracks every dollar in state politics and it's all technically public record. But accessing it means registering an account, submitting a manual request, and waiting for files to arrive by email. Here's what that looks like in practice.

Nevada campaign finance data is public.

It is also, for most people, completely out of reach.

What AURORA Actually Is

AURORA is Nevada's campaign finance disclosure system, maintained by the Secretary of State's office. Every contribution made to a Nevada candidate, PAC, or committee. Every expenditure. Every independent expenditure. It is all in there, and it is all public record.

This is not optional. Candidates and committees are legally required to file. Which means this is not just a useful dataset — it is the authoritative source of truth for Nevada political money.

If you want to understand how Nevada campaigns are funded, who is donating, how money is being deployed in a race, and where candidates are spending — this is the only place that data lives.

Why It Is Effectively Inaccessible

The problem is not that the data is hidden. The problem is how you have to go get it.

AURORA does not have a public download link. It does not have an API. To get bulk data, you have to register an account with the Nevada Secretary of State's office, navigate a separate data request portal, select which datasets you want — and then wait. Files are delivered by email or FTP, on the state's timeline, not yours.

Once the files arrive, you have six separate CSV tables: candidates, groups, reports, contacts, contributions, and expenditures. They are relational — meaning a contribution record references a contact ID, which you have to join to the contacts table to get a name and address. An expenditure references a payee ID. Reports link back to either a candidate or a group. Nothing is pre-joined or pre-aggregated. It is raw data, and it expects you to know what to do with it.

There is no built-in analytics layer. No trending. No geographic breakdowns. No way to answer "how much has this candidate raised from in-district donors" without building that logic yourself.

And every time you want fresh data, you go through the process again. There is no webhook. No automatic refresh. No way to stay current without manually repeating the request cycle.

What That Process Actually Looks Like

Let's say you are working a Nevada Assembly race and you want a basic competitive read: how much have the two candidates raised, where is the money coming from, and how are they spending?

In a world where the data is just accessible, that is a five-minute question. In Nevada's current system, here is what you are actually doing:

You register an account. You navigate to the bulk data portal. You add the datasets you need to a request queue — there is literally a shopping cart interface, for free public data. You submit. You wait for an email. You download the files when they arrive. You write or find code to join six tables. You filter for the candidates you care about. You figure out what the totals actually mean. You build the comparison yourself.

That is a process that takes hours, assuming you already know how the schema works. For a consultant managing five races at once, or a campaign staffer who just needs answers before a donor call, this is not a realistic workflow.

Who Gets Left Behind

Well-resourced campaigns and established consultants can absorb this friction. They have staff who have done it before. They have scrapers and pipelines they have already built. The upfront investment has been amortized across multiple cycles.

Everyone else is working without the data. Challengers, local races, campaigns without a full-time data person — they are making fundraising decisions, allocation calls, and spending choices without a real picture of what is happening in the race. Not because the information does not exist. Because the process of getting to it is genuinely prohibitive.

Nevada has become an increasingly competitive swing state. Its state legislative races have drawn serious money and serious outside attention. The campaigns operating in that environment deserve access to the same information their opponents may already have.
